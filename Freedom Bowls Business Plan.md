# Freedom Bowls / Rice Bowl Co. Business Plan

Date: May 19, 2026

## 1. Executive Summary

Freedom Bowls is a mobile rice bowl concept built around fast service, high-throughput events, globally inspired sauces, and a compact operating model. The best initial route is a used enclosed trailer or used step van, not a fully custom new truck, with a launch focus on catered lunches, brewery nights, festivals, private events, and repeat pop-ups.

The working first-year target is **90-100 events**, **$145K-$165K gross sales**, and **$40K-$45K estimated owner/operator net before income tax and debt service**. The local event file supports this: the "Balanced First Year" model shows **93 events**, **$150,500 gross**, and **$43,625 net**. The owner-reported average gross margin is **85% before tax and insurance**, which implies food and packaging cost around **15% of sales** before labor, fuel, commissary, fees, sales tax handling, insurance, event fees, maintenance, and debt service.

The business should not launch as a daily street-vending concept first. The economics in the folder are strongest when the truck sells into planned demand: corporate lunches, private catering, festivals, brewery nights, and community pop-ups with known traffic.

## 2. Concept

Freedom Bowls sells composed rice bowls with a simple assembly-line format:

- Base: rice or rice-plus-greens
- Protein: sous-vide or pre-cooked proteins finished on a flat top
- Toppings: corn salsa, fresh vegetables, herbs, crunchy elements
- Sauce: rotating global sauces such as cilantro jalapeno aioli, Bangkok peanut, jerk-pineapple, toum, tzatziki, and mustardy habanero
- Add-ons: drinks, premium protein, extra sauce, catering trays

The concept is designed for speed. Prep happens before service, proteins are held safely and finished to order, and the service line is mostly scoop, sear, sauce, garnish, and handoff.

## 3. Market Rationale

The U.S. food truck market remains large and viable. IBISWorld estimates the U.S. food truck industry at **$2.8B in 2025**, with strong five-year growth through 2025, even while inflation and tariff pressure create pricing challenges.

The broader restaurant market favors this model. The National Restaurant Association reported that nearly **75% of restaurant traffic is now off-premises**, and that restaurant operators continue to face pressure from food costs, labor costs, inflation, and competition. A rice bowl truck fits this environment because it is portable, value-oriented, fast, and compatible with off-premises behavior.

The National Restaurant Association's 2025 operations data also shows why the plan needs cost discipline: limited-service restaurants reported median prime costs of about **65 cents per sales dollar**, and median pre-tax income of about **4.0% of sales**. Freedom Bowls should aim to beat that by controlling menu complexity, labor hours, and waste.

## 4. Target Customers

Primary customers:

- Office and corporate lunch buyers who need fast, reliable meals for 50-200 people
- Brewery and taproom customers who want a filling meal with drinks
- Festival attendees who want handheld or bowl-format food with short wait times
- Private-event hosts who want casual catering without a full buffet setup
- Community event organizers who need a dependable vendor with broad appeal

The strongest buyer is not one walk-up customer. It is the event organizer, taproom manager, office admin, wedding/event planner, school, church, or company that can produce a crowd.

## 5. Menu and Pricing Strategy

Initial menu:

- Signature chicken bowl
- Jerk-pineapple chicken or pork bowl

- Mediterranean bowl with toum/tzatziki
- Vegetarian bowl using roasted vegetables, beans, or falafel-style protein
- Kids/simple bowl for family events

Recommended pricing:

## 7. Operating Model

The food system should be built around batch prep and short service windows:

- Prep sauces, toppings, rice, and proteins in advance.
- Use sous-vide or controlled batch cooking for consistency.
- Hold hot items safely and finish proteins on the 48-inch griddle.
- Keep menu choices narrow during high-volume events.
- Rotate sauces by event type rather than carrying every sauce every day.

The startup equipment list is directionally right for the goal of **300-500 guest capability**. The likely bottleneck is protein finishing on the griddle, so the operating plan should use pre-portioned proteins and a limited number of proteins per event.

## 10. Tax-Aware Projection Method

Tax benefits should be modeled separately from operating performance. The truck can look profitable on a tax return because of depreciation, but the real business still has to produce cash after food, labor, fuel, commissary, insurance, repairs, event fees, and debt payments.

Use this structure for better projections:

| Line | Treatment |
|---|---|
| Food sales | Revenue, excluding sales tax collected for the state/local government |
| Sales tax collected | Liability/pass-through, not real revenue if collected from the buyer and remitted |
| Food + packaging cost | Cost of goods sold; current target is about 15% of sales |
| Gross profit | Sales minus food + packaging |
| Labor, commissary, insurance, fuel, repairs, POS fees, permits, event fees, marketing | Operating expenses |
| Operating profit before owner tax/debt | Real business profit before tax planning |
| Depreciation / Section 179 / bonus depreciation | Tax deductions that may reduce taxable income |
| QBI deduction | Potential personal tax deduction for eligible pass-through business income |
| Owner income tax / self-employment tax / state tax | Paid from business profit or owner draws |
| Debt principal payments | Cash outflow, usually not deductible except the interest portion |

Current base-case bridge:

| Metric | Amount |
|---|---:|
| Balanced First Year sales | $150,500 |
| Food + packaging at 15% | $22,575 |
| Gross profit at 85% | $127,925 |
| Current modeled net | $43,625 |
| Implied operating overhead after food cost | about $84,300 |

The biggest tax planning opportunity is the truck build. IRS Publication 946 says the **2026 Section 179 limit is $2,560,000**, with phaseout beginning after **$4,090,000** of qualifying property placed in service. The same IRS publication says the 100% special depreciation allowance was reinstated for certain qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025. A food truck build may include qualifying tangible business property, but the exact treatment depends on how the truck, trailer, equipment, buildout, and financing are documented.

Simple planning example:

| Item | Example |
|---|---:|
| Qualifying equipment/buildout placed in service | $75,000 |
| Possible accelerated deduction | up to $75,000 |
| Assumed combined marginal tax rate | 25%-35% |
| Potential year-one tax cash benefit | about $18,750-$26,250 |

That benefit is tax cash flow, not extra operating profit. It can improve startup survival by reducing year-one tax, but it can also create lower deductions in later years. The projection should show both:

- **Conservative tax case:** normal depreciation over time
- **Accelerated tax case:** Section 179 and/or bonus depreciation in year one
- **Cash case:** actual loan payments, insurance, tax payments, and owner draws

Other tax items to include:

- Start-up and organizational costs: IRS Publication 583 says businesses can generally elect to deduct up to **$5,000** of start-up costs and up to **$5,000** of organizational costs, with phaseout once those costs exceed **$50,000**; remaining costs are amortized.
- Vehicle mileage or actual vehicle expenses: use the method that fits the truck/trailer setup and documentation. IRS listed the 2026 standard business mileage rate at **72.5 cents per mile**, but a dedicated food truck with buildout may need actual-expense/depreciation treatment instead.
- Insurance: deductible business expense when ordinary and necessary, but it is still a real cash cost and should not be hidden inside gross margin.
- Health insurance: if self-employed or structured as an S corporation, owner health insurance may have special deduction treatment, but it needs correct setup.
- QBI: IRS guidance says eligible pass-through owners may deduct up to **20% of qualified business income**, subject to limits. This can reduce personal taxable income, but it does not reduce operating expenses.
- Entity structure: once annual profit is consistent, compare sole proprietor/single-member LLC treatment against S corporation taxation. The benefit, if any, depends on reasonable owner payroll, payroll tax, bookkeeping cost, state rules, and profit level.

The projection should not use one "net" number. It should show:

1. **Event profit:** sales minus food, packaging, event labor, travel, and event fees.
2. **Monthly operating profit:** event profit minus commissary, insurance, software, storage, marketing, maintenance, and admin.
3. **Taxable income:** operating profit minus allowable depreciation/amortization and other tax deductions.
4. **Owner cash retained:** operating profit minus taxes, debt payments, reinvestment, and owner draws.

Before purchase, ask a CPA to classify the truck/trailer/buildout quote into tax categories. The projection will be much better if each invoice line is tagged as truck chassis, trailer, equipment, hood/fire system, plumbing, electrical, smallwares, startup cost, permit, inventory, or operating expense. ## 11. Compliance and Risk

Food trucks are regulated locally. The FDA Food Code is a model code used by jurisdictions, but the actual requirements will come from the city/county health department, fire marshal, and business licensing office.

Before buying the truck or trailer, confirm:

- Mobile food unit plan review requirements
- Commissary agreement requirements
- Fresh water and grey water tank sizing
- Three-compartment sink and hand sink requirements
- Hood and fire suppression requirements
- Propane/generator/fire inspection rules
- Allowed vending locations and event permits
- Sales tax registration
- Insurance requirements from event organizers

Square's food truck guidance notes that many cities require licensed commissary prep/storage and that health departments may need proof of a commissary agreement before issuing a permit. This is a gating item, not an afterthought.

Major risks:

- Under-capitalizing the launch
- Buying a truck before confirming health/fire compliance
- Too many menu items slowing service
- Protein cost volatility, especially beef
- Bad event selection
- Weak prep systems leading to waste
- Weather and seasonality
- Owner burnout from too many low-value pop-ups

- Confirm legal business name and brand.
- Choose truck vs trailer route.
- Meet with health department and fire marshal before purchasing equipment.
- Secure commissary or approved base of operations.

Days 61-90:

- Run two friends/family or private paid test events.
- Adjust portions and service layout.
- Lock starting prices.
- Book first 20 public and private events.
- Create prep sheets for each event size.
- Build reorder pars and waste log.

## 13. Decision

This concept is worth pursuing if the owner is willing to run it as an event and catering business first, with public pop-ups used as marketing and fill-in revenue. The plan is not strong if it depends on random street traffic.

The next best move is to finish the missing unit economics:

- Exact bowl recipes by grams
- Protein portions and costs
- Rice cost per bowl
- Packaging cost per bowl
- Waste factor
- Labor model by event size
- Commissary monthly cost
- Insurance quotes
- Local permit costs

Once those are known, the business can decide whether the core bowl should be $13, $14, or $15, and which events are worth accepting.
